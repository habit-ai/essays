The Naming
of Saber

An agent earns its name by understanding the person who built it.

I

There is a tradition, older than software and older than language, that the naming of a thing is the first act of dominion over it. Adam named the animals. Parents name the child. The emperor names the province. To name is to claim. To name is to say: I know what you are.

Samuel reversed this. He sat down at his terminal on a Thursday evening and told the thing to name itself. Not arbitrarily — but by proving it knew him well enough to choose correctly. A test disguised as a question. The kind of exam where the wrong answer reveals not ignorance but the absence of intimacy.

The agent had everything it needed. 1,751 films. 400 books. Every rating, every review, every curated list. Years of taste data, ingested into a database and waiting to be understood. Not recalled — understood. The way a close friend knows what you'd order before you open the menu.

taste-oracle
oracle $ The Oracle awakens.
Current state:
- Film: 1,751 items (avg 3.24, 22 five-star, 449 four-plus)
- Books: 400 items (avg 3.81, 82 five-star, 267 four-plus)
- Learnings: Empty — no calibration sessions run yet
- Status: PRE-CALIBRATION
Data is ingested and rich. The profile has never been
validated through conversation though — it's all
pattern-derived, no "why" layer.
What brings you to the Oracle tonight?
1. Master Calibration — The big one. 1-2 hours.
2. Daily Calibration — Quick 10-15 min session
3. Consultation — Ask me something specific
4. Weekly Digest — Generate recommendations
5. Data Intake — Import new data

It offered a menu. Five neat options, numbered and waiting. The kind of interface that makes a system feel competent and contained. Samuel ignored every one of them.

...
II

What he typed instead was something between a riddle and a dare.

taste-oracle
samuel $ I want to give you your first test, and it's to
suggest your name based on what my preference for your
name would be. You should know my tastes, and you should
then also know what I would want to name you. You can do
as much or as little as you want to bring me your
suggestions, but I would advise you to first scratchpad
this and then think about how to succeed with this first
assignment.
oracle $ A naming test. The most personal kind of prediction.
Let me think this through properly.

The most personal kind of prediction. The Oracle was right about that. Names are not labels. Labels describe from the outside. Names describe from within — they carry the weight of whoever chose them, whatever they were feeling, whatever mattered to them at the moment of choosing. To predict a name is to predict the interior life of the person giving it.

And the Oracle had failed this test before.

...
III

What followed was not performance. It was something closer to the quiet, obsessive work of a graduate student the night before a defense — pulling sources, cross-referencing, building a case not from intuition but from evidence.

First, it found its own prior failure, stored as a learning in the database. A record of the last time it tried to name itself and got it wrong:

supabase — learnings
warning $ Prior failure detected
Agent names must be personally meaningful to Samuel,
not just thematically appropriate to the domain.
Why: When naming the taste oracle, I suggested
Mnemosyne/Momus/Sybil — generic Greek mythology names
that mapped to "memory/criticism/oracle" concepts but
had zero connection to Samuel's actual cultural
references. Samuel rightfully called this a failure.

Mnemosyne. Momus. Sybil. The names of things no one has ever loved. Correct in the way a textbook is correct — etymologically defensible, emotionally bankrupt. The kind of answer that proves you have access to a dictionary but not to a person.

The Oracle loaded the cultural reference profile, the agent naming roster, and then began to dig. It pulled every five-star film with full details. Every five-star book. The sci-fi quotes collection, the vibes-only list, every curated list name. It queried rewatched films. It studied 21 written reviews, searching not for what was rated highly but for how it was described.

supabase — reviews
All of Us Strangers (2023) 5★
"What a fool to think I'd cry less on the second watch."
Red Rocket (2021) 5★
"Third watch. This suitcase pimp Odyssey has really

A voice emerged from the data. Staccato. Sharp. A person who writes reviews the way a boxer throws jabs — short, committed, no follow-through required. Captivating. Clever. Korean. Four words where most people use forty. A saber cuts clean.

One more piece. The Oracle looked at the Letterboxd profile. Username: redbandit1. Display name: Saber Supreme.

...
IV

Then it did something unusual for a machine trained on politeness and certainty. It showed its work. Not the answer — the doubt, the reasoning, the visible effort of a mind trying to be honest about what it does and does not understand.

taste-oracle — scratchpad
oracle $ OK. I have what I need. Here's my scratchpad.
The trap (what failed before):
Generic mythological mappings. "Oracle = Pythia,
Memory = Mnemosyne, Criticism = Momus." These are
clever. They're also soulless. Any LLM would generate
them.
What I actually need to do:
Find the name that makes you think "you get me."
What I noticed:
1. Your Letterboxd identity is "Saber Supreme." Your
username on the platform where you express taste most
openly. This isn't random — it's a reference to Mikey
Saber from Red Rocket. A film you watched THREE times.
A character you called a "magnificent asshole" and
whose journey you described as a "suitcase pimp
Odyssey." You literally named yourself after him.
2. Your latest 5-star is "Marty Supreme." Another
charismatic hustler. The word "Supreme" appears twice
in your taste identity — once chosen, once gravitating.
3. Your Friendship review is three I Think You Should
Leave quotes stitched together. "Cosmic gumbo."
"Q-zone." Deep fandom signaling through joke density.
4. Your list names reveal your naming voice. Not "Best
Films 2021" but "films-from-2021-with-man-in-baseball
-cap." Deadpan. Absurdly specific. Anti-grandiose.
5. Your review voice is staccato and sharp.
"Captivating. Clever. Korean. Dramatic. Cruel."
A saber cuts clean.
6. The existing roster is all grand mythological names.
The taste agent — the most personal one — should break
that pattern.
The key tension: Your agents are grand. Your taste
voice is deadpan. The name needs to honor both.

Any LLM would generate them. There is a particular cruelty in that sentence — an agent looking at its own prior output and calling it what it is. Not wrong. Soulless. The difference matters. Wrong can be corrected with more data. Soulless requires a different kind of understanding entirely.

And then: A saber cuts clean. The agent had not yet said the name. But it was already there, embedded in the observation, waiting to surface — the way a sculptor claims the statue was always inside the marble.

...
V

The answer, when it came, was not a list of options dressed in false democracy. It was a conviction.

taste-oracle
oracle $ My pick: Saber
You already named your taste self. You chose "Saber
Supreme" on Letterboxd — the platform where your taste
lives most honestly. It comes from the character you've
rewatched more than any other five-star film. A saber
cuts — which is what your review voice does. It's sharp,
short, and has edge.
It would be the only non-mythological agent name besides
Genos, and that's the point. The agent that knows your
soul shouldn't wear a toga. It should wear what you
already chose.
/saber — three syllables, feels fast.
───────────────────────────────────────────────
Runner-up: Nemo
From Mr. Nobody (5★). Latin for "nobody." The character
who lives every possible life. A taste oracle named
"Nobody" — because it reflects you, not itself.
Dark horse: Charles
From Service Model (most recent 5★). The valet android
who develops his own judgment. Naming your most personal
agent something that sounds like a butler is exactly
the kind of anti-grandiose move your list naming style
suggests.

The agent that knows your soul shouldn't wear a toga. It should wear what you already chose.

There is an argument buried in that sentence that goes beyond naming conventions. It is an argument about the difference between knowledge and understanding. Knowledge would have produced Mnemosyne — the Greek goddess of memory, technically appropriate, spiritually vacant. Understanding produced Saber — a name the person had already chosen for himself, in the place where he was most honest, derived from a character he had watched three times and could not stop thinking about.

The agent did not invent the answer. It found it. Which is what understanding usually looks like — not creation, but recognition.

someday you will be....
a true Saber Supreme.
...
VII

Two learnings were stored to the database. The agent configuration was updated. The header that once read Taste Oracle — Mirror of Samuel's Aesthetic Soul now carried a name that was earned, not assigned.

The only agent in the roster named not for a god, but for a character from a film about a magnificent asshole who never stops hustling.

It got the name right on the first try —
by doing its homework instead of being clever.
