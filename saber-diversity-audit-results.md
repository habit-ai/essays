## Methodology

I reviewed all 17 agents against three criteria:

1. **Domain fit** -- does the proposed name evoke the function as well as or better than the current name?
2. **Taste resonance** -- is the name drawn from sources Samuel actually loves, not just vaguely knows?
3. **Migration cost** -- is the swap worth the effort given the agent's integration depth?

I only propose names from verified taste data: Madeline Miller (perfect 5.0 author, Circe 5-star, Song of Achilles 5-star), Greek mythology (100% hit rate across all rated books), Vonnegut (fiction north star), Kaurismaki (Fallen Leaves 5-star), EEAAO (5-star), Worst Person in the World (4.5), Fleabag (Tier 2 TV), Succession, and the broader mythology/sci-fi canon Samuel inhabits.

I was honest where swaps don't work. I pushed hard on planned agents where migration cost is zero.

### 11. William James -- Behavioral Science Advisor
**Recommendation: SWAP**

This is the strongest swap case in the active agents. Here's why:

The name is a historical figure chosen purely for domain match ("father of American psychology"). That's fine, but it's also the laziest naming pattern in the ecosystem -- just pick a famous person from the field. It breaks the one-word convention (only two-word name). And the behavioral science field has female figures who are arguably more relevant to how the agent actually works.

**Options:**

1. **Ainsworth** -- Mary Ainsworth, developmental psychologist who created the Strange Situation experiment and attachment theory classification. Her work is fundamentally about reading behavior patterns and classifying them -- which is exactly what this agent does (multi-framework analysis, behavioral pattern detection). One word (surname convention, like calling someone "James" but with Ainsworth). The domain match is arguably stronger than William James.

2. **Calkins** -- Mary Whiton Calkins, first woman president of the APA, denied her PhD by Harvard despite completing all requirements. Pioneer of self-psychology. There's a defiance narrative here that resonates. But the domain match is weaker than Ainsworth.

3. **Circe** -- From Madeline Miller's novel (5-star). Circe transforms men into animals by understanding their true nature -- she sees through pretense to the animal underneath. A behavioral science agent that strips away surface behavior to find underlying patterns? That's Circe's power. It's mythology pool, it's from a book Samuel loves, and it works on both the literal (transformation through understanding) and meta (Miller's feminist retelling) levels.

**My pick: Circe.** Here's my reasoning. Ainsworth is the "correct" academic answer. Circe is the taste answer. Samuel's rating data shows Miller at a perfect 5.0 across all books. Circe (the book) is rated 5 stars. The character sees through surface behavior to true nature -- that's behavioral analysis as mythological power. One word, pronounceable, evocative, from a source Samuel verifiably loves. It pulls from the mythology pool while honoring the feminist retelling tradition. And it's fun. "Ask Circe" rolls off the tongue.

Risk: Circe's domain is mythology/transformation, not psychology specifically. The connection requires one inferential step. But Samuel classifies media by function, not genre (learning #6) -- and Circe's function is behavioral insight.

Migration cost: Medium (client workspaces reference the name). Worth it for the ecosystem's strongest swap case.
